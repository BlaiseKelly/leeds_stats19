STATS19 reported road casualties: LA of Leeds

Published

March 5, 2026

Introduction

This is a report for the Local Authority (LA) region of Leeds, which can be reproduced for any LA by cloning the repository at https://github.com/blaisekelly/stats19_stats. #The LA boundary is shown on this plot of the cycle network (from OSM data processed with the osmactive package).

In this report all serious and slight casualty data is based on the adjusted data.

In 2024, Leeds had 1461 collisions involving all casualty types resulting in 1896 casualties reported to the police. This is the 2nd highest of 374 Local Authorities in Great Britain (98th per capita) and an increase in collisions of 2 and an increase in total casualties of 16. For fatal it ranked 21st ( 216th per capita) with 11 casualties down on 2023. For KSIs it ranked 2nd (92nd per capita) with 459 casualties down on 2023. For serious injuries it ranked 2nd (83rd per 1000) with 448 casualties down on 2023. For slight injuries it ranked 2nd (101st per 1000) with 1437 casualties up on 2023.

Compared to 5 years ago collisions are up by 450, total casualties up by 632, fatalities down by 0, KSI casualties up by 151, serious injuries up by 151 and slight injuries up by 481.

For cyclists there were 219 collisions resulting in 222 cyclist casualties reported to the police. This is the 9th highest of 374 Local Authorities in Great Britain (57th per capita) and a decrease in collisions of 33 but a decrease in total casualties. For fatal it ranked 6th (58th per capita) with 2 cyclist casualties up on 2023. For KSIs it ranked 6th ( 72nd per capita) with 65 casualties down on 2023. For serious injuries it ranked 6th (75th per 1000) with 63 cyclist casualties down on 2023. For slight injuries it ranked 10th (57th per 1000) with 157 cyclist casualties down on 2023.

Compared to 5 years ago collisions involving cyclists are up by 1, total casualties up by 1, fatalities up by 1, KSI casualties down by 4, serious injuries down by 5 and slight injuries up by 5.

For pedestrians there were 313 collisions resulting in 318 pedestrian casualties reported to the police. This is the 3rd highest of 374 Local Authorities in Great Britain (55th per capita) and an increase in collisions of 9 and an increase in total casualties. For fatal it ranked 15th (154th per capita) with 4 pedestrian casualties down on 2023. For KSIs it ranked 5th (68th per capita) with 99 casualties down on 2023. For serious injuries it ranked 5th (70th per capita) with 95 pedestrian casualties down on 2023. For slight injuries it ranked 3rd (56th per capita) with 219 pedestrian casualties up on 2023.

Compared to 5 years ago collisions involving pedestrians are up by 130, total casualties up by 128, fatalities up by 3, KSI casualties up by 28, serious injuries up by 25 and slight injuries up by 100.

Speed Limits: Casualty rates by road speed limit

In Leeds, according to OSM data the majority of roads are 20mph (22%). Between 2020 and 2024 the majority of collisions (3997) happened on 30mph roads. (40)% of fatalities happened on 30mph roads, which make up 12% of roads in the LA. 1183 casualties were seriously injured on 30mph roads. Collision rates per km are highest for 30mph roads, fatality rates highest on 60mph roads, serious injury rates highest on 30mph and slight injury rates 30mph. The collision rate on 20mph roads was 10.9% lower than the highest roads (30mph) and fatality rates 12.5% of 30mph roads and 6.2% of the highest fatality rate (60mph) roads. KSI rates were highest on 30mph roads, 8.1 times higher than 20mph roads.

Relationship with Index of Multiple Deprivation Decile (IMD)

The Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) is the official measure of relative deprivation in England and is part of a suite of outputs that form the Indices of Deprivation (IoD) given as a decile in the STATS19 casualty and vehicle data for the casualty and drivers home LSOA respectively. It follows an established methodological framework in broadly defining deprivation to encompass a wide range of an individual’s living conditions. It is important in road traffic collision analysis because it says something about where harm is distributed in society. In Leeds, 50.6% of the population fall into the most deprived IMD decile. 68.1% of casualties injured between 2020 and 2024 fall into the most deprived IMD decile. 96.3% of casualty data had home LSOA.

IMD and Casualties

The relationship between deprivation and road casualty risk is marked in Leeds. The most deprived IMD decile (decile 1) accounts for 32.2% of casualties (2751), while the least deprived (decile 10) accounts for 5.4% (462). Residents of the most deprived areas are 6 times more likely to be a casualty than those in the least deprived decile. The decile with the highest number of casualties overall is decile 1 with 202 casualties (2.4%). As noted above, 96.3% of casualty records had a home LSOA that could be matched to an IMD decile, where as only xx of driver data had data on home location.

The charts below show a breakdown of the ages and sex number of KSI and IMD Decil of casualties in Leeds.

Analysis of KSI casualties by IMD decile in Leeds reveals that decile 1 (most deprived) accounts for the highest KSI total across casualty types combined, with approximately 670 KSI casualties. Car occupants represent the highest KSI casualty type overall (~883). Pedestrian KSI peaks in decile 1, while cyclist KSI is highest in decile 1. By age, the most affected groups are 0-11 for pedestrians, 30-39 for cyclists, and 16-19 for motorcyclists. Males account for approximately 1357 KSI casualties compared to 648 for females.

MSOA

Between LSOA and Local Authority are Medium Super Output Levels (MSOA). Because of their relatively consistent size across the country, they are a useful unit for analysing data about small local areas and they also have been allocated descriptive names by the House of Commons Library makming it easier to describe areas. These areas have been used to analyse casualties by MSOA region and compare at a country level, similar to as has been done with LAs at the beginning of this document, but hopefully providing more targeted information for a local area.

Analysing the population weighted IMD from LSOA for MSOA for different casualty types, the strongest trends are for Pedestrian, Motorcyclist, Car occupant casualties as shown in the plot below.

The table below shows data for the 107 MSOA areas in Leeds where casualties live and ranked against other MSOAs nationally. Casualties involved in collisions outside of Leeds are also included. Between 2020 and 2024, the MSOA with the highest number of casualties was Harehills South with 194 casualties. The most deprived MSOA (by weighted IMD score) was Seacroft North & Monkswood (IMD 10), while the least deprived was Alwoodley (IMD 1). The highest MSOA regions per capita where casualties live in Leeds are Rothwell Inner, ranked 31 nationally out of 7264 for fatalities per capita, Belle Isle North ranked 1 for KSIs, Belle Isle North ranked 1 for Serious casualties.

Casualty Demographics

Between 2020 and 2024, 62.6% of all casualties in Leeds were male and 37.2% were female. Males also accounted for 68% of KSI casualties. The most common age band for casualties was 30-39 (19.3% of all casualties), while the highest KSI rate was among the 30-39 age band (16.8% of all KSI). Cost of prevention

Government TAG methodology (described here and here allocates a cost of prevention to each collision. The costs are calculated initially based on casualty severity and then grouped by collision severity. For example, the cost of fatal collisions includes the cost of all casualties (including serious and slight casualties) that occur in fatal collisions. This can be matched in various ways to the collision data. The two methods used for the overall reporting in ONS is to simply match the collision severity recorded in the crash data to a single cost for slight, serious and fatal and also to match based on severity and road type. Using the same methods as in the ONS report, the chart below shows the total cost of prevention for the collisions Leeds.

The cost of collisions by vehicle involved and by speed limit are shown in the plot below. 30mph roads had the highest total cost with £475,394,972, 183.5% above 40mph. The lowest total cost were 50mph with £28,793,838

Dividing the total cost on each road by the number of collisions gives an estimate for the average cost of each collision on each road. The lowest (£118,938) were 30mph roads and the highest (£210,479) 60mph roads. 58% of total collisions took place on 30mph roads, but 53% of the total cost and 60mph roads had 4% of total collisions but 7% of the total cost. The cost of collisions on 60mph roads are, on average, 1.8 times higher than 30mph roads. The average cost of collisions on 30mph roads was 0.9 times higher than 20mph roads.

Single Vehicle Pavement Collisions

Between 2020 and 2024, nationally there were 9327 single-vehicle collisions where a pedestrian was struck on a footway or verge. 81% were people driving Cars, Taxis, Goods vehicles and Buses and 12% were people riding pedal cycles and e-scooters. Of these collisions there were 138 fatalities, 94% of these fatal collisions were with people driving Cars, Taxis, Goods vehicles and Buses and 1% died from collisions with people riding pedal cycles and e-scooters. A further 2300 serious injuries, 79% of the fatal collisions were with people driving Cars, Taxis, Goods vehicles and Buses and 13% from collisions with people riding pedal cycles and e-scooters.

In Leeds there were 119 reported incidents, resulting in 124 casualties. The vehicle type most commonly involved was Car with 66 collisions and 70 casualties. 86% of the vehicles in collisions were people driving Cars, Taxis, Goods vehicles and Buses and 4% were people riding pedal cycles and escooters. Of these collisions there were 3 fatalities, 100% of these collisions were with people driving Cars, Taxis, Goods vehicles and Buses and 0% from collisions with people riding pedal cycles and e-scooters. A further 26 serious injuries, 77% of which were with people driving Cars, Taxis, Goods vehicles and Buses and 0% from collisions with people riding pedal cycles and e-scooters.
